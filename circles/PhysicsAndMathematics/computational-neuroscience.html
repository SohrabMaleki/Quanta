Computational Neuroscience

Lecturer: Ashkan Damavandi

Computational neuroscience is an interdisciplinary field that combines physiology and neurobiology, physics, mathematics, and computer science to understand and analyze the mechanisms of the brain. By using data analysis, mathematical modeling, and simulation methods, this field examines processes of the brain at multiple scales—from the dynamics of individual neurons and synapses to emergent behaviors of neural networks such as decision-making, environmental adaptation, and sensory processing.

In this study circle, we will proceed step by step, starting from basic and introductory topics, to become familiar with modeling and simulation tools in computational neuroscience. Given the breadth and depth of the field, we will devote extra time to more important topics when necessary in order to maximize learning and effectiveness.


References: Computational neuroscience encompasses a wide range of topics, each with its own significance. As such, a single comprehensive reference that adequately covers all areas is either insufficient or rarely available. Therefore, throughout the circle we will use and introduce different sources for different topics. We will begin with two main books: Dynamical Systems in Neuroscience by Izhikevich and Neuronal Dynamics by Gerstner. In addition to textbooks and lecture notes, we will also review a variety of reputable research papers.


Prerequisites: Biological and physiological aspects of neuroscience—such as biochemical mechanisms of the nervous system and the function of neurons—will be introduced as needed during the sessions. Therefore, prior knowledge in these areas is not required. For better engagement with the material, minimal familiarity with differential equations, basic probability, general physics, and introductory Python programming is very useful. In addition, a general background in statistical mechanics and linear algebra can be helpful. However, whenever necessary, we will provide quick reviews of prerequisites and recommend supplementary resources.

3. Mathematical Foundations of Modelling in Neuroscience (II)

Quanta Study Circles, SUT – 13 August 2025

The Hodgkin–Huxley and Morris–Lecar models examine the role of ion channels in neuronal membranes and their effect on changes in the membrane’s electrical potential. The Pinsky–Rinzel model studies neural spikes in the soma and dendrite of pyramidal neurons in the hippocampus, the Kuramoto model addresses the collective activity of neurons, and the Wilson–Cowan model investigates the interactions between populations of neurons. In this session, we will continue exploring concepts of dynamical systems using these models.

  • Analysis of attractors, equilibrium, and chaotic behavior in the Hodgkin–Huxley and Pinsky–Rinzel models
  • Stability and equilibrium in the Kuramoto phase model
  • Lyapunov function and exponent in the LIF model
  • Investigation of bifurcations in the Wilson–Cowan and Morris–Lecar models

Notes

6. The Modelling of Epilepsy (I)

Quanta Study Circles, SUT – 14 September 2025

In the upcoming sessions of the circle, we will begin examining epilepsy as an important case study for modeling neural networks. In computational neuroscience, models must be validated and checked for accuracy by comparing their results with electrophysiological data. One type of data commonly used for this purpose is EEG.

In this session, after introducing epilepsy and EEG data, we will move on to comparing and analyzing the results of the Wilson–Cowan and Jansen–Rit models. Finally, we will discuss the importance of stochastic fluctuations and noise in modeling, as a way to achieve better agreement with real-world data.

  • Examining the importance of epilepsy in neuroscience
  • Introduction to EEG data
  • Dynamical analysis of the Wilson–Cowan model (continuation from the previous session)
  • Dynamical analysis of the Jansen–Rit model
  • Stochastic fluctuations in modeling
