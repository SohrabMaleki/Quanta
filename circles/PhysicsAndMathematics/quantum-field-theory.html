Quantum Field Theory

Lecturer: Mohammad Amin Sadeghian

In this study circle, we aim for a deeper and more fundamental perspective on Quantum Field Theory (QFT), both from the physical and the mathematical standpoint. Quantum Field Theory is an extensive field with a wide range of topics, where even a thorough examination of a single subject can take months or years. Nevertheless, in the standard way it is usually taught, many important aspects are presented without sufficient depth and richness. This often leads to serious difficulties in building the necessary foundation for understanding more modern and advanced areas of physics. Taking this limitation into account, we will begin with the basics and gradually move toward more advanced and contemporary topics in field theory. Whenever we feel that a discussion or topic requires deeper reflection, we will refer to additional sources and papers. However, in order to avoid excessive perfectionism and unnecessary slowdown, we will follow one or at most two main textbooks as our primary references and framework.


References: From the physical perspective, the circle’s main reference will be Fradkin’s book. Its strength lies in its effort to provide a comprehensive picture of field theory as a framework applied throughout physics—not only in the standard computations of scattering amplitudes and Feynman diagrams for elementary particles, but also in shaping the physicist’s broader intuition. From the mathematical perspective, the references will be either Talagrand or Hamilton, with the relative weight depending on how much mathematical emphasis the circle chooses to pursue.


Prerequisites: Participants are expected to be familiar with Quantum Mechanics, Special Relativity, and to some extent Classical Field Theory. A brief and preliminary familiarity with Quantum Field Theory would also be helpful in following the sessions, though it is not strictly required.
