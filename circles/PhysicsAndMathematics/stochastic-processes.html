Stochastic Processes

Lecturer: Hooman Zare

“The gap between physical intuition and mathematical rigor is where demons hide.” — Hermann Weyl


Stochastic (or random) processes are one of those fields where mathematics and physics are forced to advance only by helping one another. In such contexts, Hermann Weyl’s warning is often the most central and most difficult part of the story. Therefore, the aim of these sessions is to take a closer look at situations where our physical intuition might mislead us.

In these meetings, we will begin by exploring both the traditional and newer intuitions surrounding these processes, and use them to introduce the topic from an interdisciplinary perspective. Then, we will tackle a number of modern problems, and finally turn to problems that we once thought we had solved—or still think we have solved correctly—and show how experiments, computer simulations, relations, and even laws can break down! Afterwards, with the insights we have gained, we will attempt to formulate research directions that can push open problems a step further.


References: Stochastic processes are both extremely detailed and, at the same time, highly reducible. For this reason, references may range from undergraduate physics texts to books in pure mathematics; thus, the best approach is to learn “on demand.” However, if you wish to pursue research in this area from an applied perspective, it is recommended to use a comprehensive book that does not follow a strict proof-based style yet is far from simplistic. One of the best books that follows such an approach is Dr. Rahimi Tabar’s Analysis and Data-Based Reconstruction of Complex Nonlinear Dynamical Systems.


Prerequisites: Since most prerequisites for these discussions are not part of the standard curriculum, they will be addressed within the sessions themselves. Familiarity with statistics and probability, as well as general ideas about entropy, partition functions, and Brownian motion, can be helpful. Overall, the most important skill required is the ability to follow the discussion—even when the details are not entirely clear!

1. An Introduction to Stochastic Processes

Quanta Study Circles, SUT – 28 July 2025

Building on the previous session, in this circle we will begin with a distribution function, construct stochastic processes based on known statistics, and then introduce one of the most famous and powerful tools of discrete stochastic systems: the Master equation. Using this tool, we can solve many modern problems and study well-known papers across various fields.

The core concepts of this session will be stationarity tests, the Master equation, and its equivalents in other disciplines (for example: Fermi levels, the Lindblad equation for open quantum systems, chemical reaction equations, gene expression equations, reduced density matrices in cosmological fields, SIR and population models, the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation in control theory, agent-based modeling, hidden states in CTMCs in computer science, Redfield relaxation theory in spin resonance, Förster/Dexter transfer in condensed matter physics, and more).

After proving the theorems, we will try to examine these examples in detail and learn the main idea of how the Master equation is applied in each of these fields. Finally, by introducing a curious example of a chemical balance that created major challenges in dynamical systems, we will put this theory to the test, solve it, and eliminate one of the common assumptions usually made in solving any Master equation.

For this session, the following concepts are required, but they will not be introduced in detail. If needed, it is recommended to consult the relevant Wikipedia pages or use AI assistance:

  1. Matrix calculus.
  2. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors.
  3. Solving the equation dx/dt = Ax, where A is a matrix and x a vector.
  4. Commutators and anticommutators.
  5. Derivative, partial derivative, integral.
  6. Dirac delta, its integral, and its cumulative distribution function.

2. The Master Equation

Quanta Study Circles, SUT – 01 August 2025

One of the key tools in studying systems with a large number of agents is the Master equation, which describes the evolution of the probability distribution of the entire system’s state. This equation finds applications across many domains, from statistical physics to economics; however, in this session, the focus will be on its role in modeling games with many players and in reinforcement learning.

On the other hand, the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman (HJB) equation lies at the heart of optimal control methods. By describing the value function of a decision-making policy, it enables finding the optimal trajectory for each agent. In stochastic environments and multi-agent games, combining the HJB framework with mean-field approaches leads to an extended Master equation, which guides decision optimization at the population level.

To make these concepts more tangible, we will consider a simple example of the Snakes and Ladders game. In the classical version, a player’s movement is determined solely by the dice; but once decision-making is introduced, the problem changes entirely.

Exercise: You can write a program that, by calculating probabilities, determines from each square of Snakes and Ladders the average number of dice rolls required to finish the game. Independently, you can simulate 1,000 runs of the game and observe the statistics; then extend this number to 1,000,000 runs to see how the averages behave.

5. Measure Theory and Statistics

Quanta Study Circles, SUT – 15 August 2025

In this session, we will focus on the mathematical foundations of stochastic analysis and the theory of stochastic processes. The goal is to make all definitions precise, introduce the key concepts, and prove some of the well-known theorems in this area.

This session is primarily aimed at pure mathematics students or those with a strong interest in these topics, since—unlike the previous sessions—most of the discussion will be devoted to analysis, measure theory, and set theory.
