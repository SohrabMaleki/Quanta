J. S. Bell (1964) - On The Einstein Podolski Rosen Paradox

05 August, 2025

Abstract

The paper before us is undoubtedly one of the most important and influential works in the foundations of quantum mechanics. To a large extent, it was written in response to a famous article by Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen, in which they argued that quantum mechanics could not be considered a complete theory of physical reality. They suggested the possibility of hidden variables that might explain why quantum mechanics, in their view, only gave an incomplete description of nature.

In his reply, John Bell showed in this groundbreaking article that no theory of hidden variables—at least of the local kind envisioned by Einstein—could reproduce all the predictions of quantum mechanics. Bell’s theorem demonstrated that the structure of quantum theory itself has experimental consequences that cannot be mimicked by any classical hidden-variable model. This established a fundamental test between quantum mechanics and local realism.

In this session, in addition to discussing Bell’s paper, we will examine its historical context and conceptual significance, explaining why it is often regarded as one of the most profound turning points in the philosophy of science and physics. We will also explore the experimental confirmations of Bell’s theorem, as well as the alternative arguments and debates that have emerged in its wake.

Lecturer: Mobin Naderi
