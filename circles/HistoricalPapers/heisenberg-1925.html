W. Heisenberg (1925) - Quantum-Theoretical Re-Interpretation of Kinematic and Mechanical Relations

29 July, 2025

Abstract

On July 29, 1925, the young Werner Heisenberg wrote a paper that marked the beginning of a great revolution in physics—an upheaval that set aside the classical concepts of mechanics and motion, and introduced a new world built on matrices, discontinuities, and strange yet precise ideas. Heisenberg’s work, known today as the birth of matrix mechanics, was the first consistent formulation of quantum mechanics. Unlike earlier attempts, it abandoned direct reference to unobservable electron orbits and instead focused only on measurable quantities, such as spectral lines. By representing physical observables as arrays of numbers (matrices) rather than simple numerical values, Heisenberg discovered the non-commutative structure of quantum theory, encapsulated later in the famous commutation relation \( [x, p] = i\hbar \). This marked a decisive shift from the smooth, deterministic picture of classical physics to a probabilistic framework that could accurately capture atomic phenomena.

In this session, on the anniversary of the publication of this paper, we will turn our attention to that exact historical moment—a moment when Heisenberg replaced the comfortable mental images of classical mechanics with something that could not be visualized in the old way. What emerged from his efforts was a new kind of mathematics: matrix mechanics, the earliest form of quantum mechanics, which was later completed and expanded by the works of Born and Jordan.

Lecturer: Sohrab Maleki
